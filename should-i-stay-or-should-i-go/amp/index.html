Should I stay or should I go

Nicole Berdy - 08 Jul 2021

(Please note that this post is probably most applicable to folks in tech, because that is the industry I have been in for the last decade. If you are not in tech and reading this going, "Wow, this lady has no idea what she's talking about," you're probably right! I don't know your industry!)

There comes a point in any job where you find yourself thinking what was perhaps once previously unthinkable: should you start looking for another role?

Before brushing up the resume, ask yourself a basic question: does your manager know you are unhappy, and if so, why do you think they know? (I am of course assuming you have a good manager who wants happy employees and will work to fix issues.) Don't assume that your unhappiness is obvious to anyone besides yourself because people tend to be caught up in their own lives and need to be clued in when they ought to pay attention. Most good managers are willing to help you if they know what you need, and you owe it to yourself to try. And if you're scared of having the conversation, think: what do you stand to lose by speaking up?

Once you realize you are not happy where you are currently, make sure you have a conversation with your manager about how you are feeling and have a clear ask for them to try to make things better. Don't wait until you are already out the door to tell them you are unhappy; you owe it to yourself to speak up. Understand whether what you want to change is feasible, have a clear and actionable ask, and know how long you are willing to wait to see change. You may need to repeat the request several times and follow up and basically spend energy you don't have. Yes, this will be hard. However, this is also a great way to ensure you don't burn any bridges on your way out.

What if you have the conversation with your manager, you try to make changes, and nothing actually happens? You need to know what you are willing to put up with and what will cause you to immediately start looking for a new job. My mentor calls the latter "ripcord moments" because they cause you to pull the ripcord to parachute out of that situation. I recommend having these written out BEFORE shit hits the fan, although it's never too late to write these down if you're already feeling stuck.

I want to share my ripcord moments with you in the hopes that you get some use from them. They reflect what I value from my work and my team; your mileage may vary. Please also note that you don't need to have a ripcord moment to change teams - you can move due to a better opportunity, better pay, horoscope reading, whatever.

Ripcord #1: People
I want my primary projects to be with people who "fill me up" with energy when I work with them. For example, I once worked with an engineering lead who brainstormed feature ideas with me between customer meetings during an international customer tour; he and I worked together exceptionally well and it was a joy to build a service with him.

My ripcord is when I work primarily with people who drain me of energy. For example, people who go directly to my skip level manager with ideas on my space drain me of energy.

Ripcord #2: Product
I want to work on something worthwhile that also makes progress. This includes making progress on existing products as well as new endeavors.

My ripcord is seeing the same known problems repeatedly go unaddressed, and current in-flight initiatives take a longer time than expected for unknown/unspecified reasons.

Ripcord #3: Team + Leadership
This is about my directs plus my leadership chain and is really 3-in-1.

My directs: Is my team appropriately funded, do I like having them work for me (did I choose them?). I don't have a ripcord on this one because I've been blessed with directs I love.

My manager: I want to feel trusted, supported, and valued. This can come as actions taken on my behalf, sponsorship, and via project assignment. Ripcord: no actions taken to make mutually acknowledged problems go away.

My senior leadership: I want to see my leaders take feedback from the team and act on it. I want them to have a valued position within the broader organization, and I want them to be able to make hard calls and explain them. Ripcord: leaders to not take action on known issues within the team, leaders cannot explain the team direction and strategy.
