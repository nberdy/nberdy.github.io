This often comes up in conversations around switching teams and the sorts of things one should look for when deciding to switch teams. It's a tough question to answer because the path to figuring out what to look for in a job or a manager is such an individual one. I posit that it's actually easier to give one-and-done advice on what NOT to look for in a job/career more easily than it is to coach on how to figure out what you DO want, but that tends to not be the most helpful. I have an analogy that might help.

Around the time you start to really ask what you need in a job to be fulfilled, and furthermore how are you supposed to figure that out, you are going through the job equivalent of early high school fashion. It's awkward, mistakes are made, too many baggy jeans are bought, but at the end you come out the other side more confident in the choices you make.

The important thing through all of this is to be true to yourself, whoever you are at the time. If you always let someone else dress you (take the jobs other people tell you to), or always wear what looks "cool" even though you're not really into it (do what your peers do), that's how you end up unhappily in a mid-life crisis. That's when you have to do the career equivalent of Marie Kondo-ing your wardrobe, holding each aspect of your job in your hands and asking if it truly sparks joy. You might find something unexpected and switch careers to run a bed-and-breakfast in your hometown. Or you might determine that your current job only needs minor adjustments to bring you more joy.

As a kid, I heard about "practice" in two ways: as the name of the activity I did after school (swim practice), and in the adage "practice makes perfect." The noun phrase "swim practice" divorced other definitions of the work "practice" from the activity - swim practice was something I did after school and I incidentally got faster and better, but the activity was the primary goal.

The adage is actually what held me up for years. I freely admit that I like being good at things - who doesn't? The idea that you can ever be perfect at something is very appealing - that you can get an award that says "perfect at checkers" or whatever, and then you're done and can move on to the next thing. But that's not how skill development actually works. True skill development is about constantly seeking out ways to better those skills without an award at the end to let you know you're done. Per Carol Dweck's fabulous book Mindset, a growth mindset is the framework of constantly looking ahead to learn and improve. Practice makes perfect is, to me, closed mindset because there is an end state of perfect that's possible. But in the real world there's no such thing as perfect.

(This is related to why I feel like it is a Very Bad Idea to praise kids for being smart instead of for trying. Possibly a topic for another time.)

Starting to learn new skills is really tough! It's easy to set lofty goals only to fall splat to the ground when you realize you flew too close to the sun in trying to achieve them. I had an old manager who talked about creating "most embarrassingly minimal viable products" in order to kickstart product iteration and innovation to keep the team from overthinking product designs. I like it as a way to think about picking up a new skill: show off and practice every smidgen of the skill you're learning, to revel in it, and embrace that while you may not be great at it today, you are better than you were yesterday.

What I'm getting at is this: I challenge you to take an agile mindset to learning something new, to treat every time you practice the skill as an iteration on your abilities. Glory in the teeny tiny steps you are taking towards learning something new, and pivot if that's where your heart takes you just so long as you're learning. And banish "practice makes perfect" from your lexicon of inspirational phrases, because it is anything but inspirational.

As you grow, you'll probably want to start inviting team members and collaborators to your site. Ghost has a number of different user roles for your team:

Contributors
This is the base user level in Ghost. Contributors can create and edit their own draft posts, but they are unable to edit drafts of others or publish posts. Contributors are untrusted users with the most basic access to your publication.

Authors
Authors are the 2nd user level in Ghost. Authors can write, edit and publish their own posts. Authors are trusted users. If you don't trust users to be allowed to publish their own posts, they should be set as Contributors.

Editors
Editors are the 3rd user level in Ghost. Editors can do everything that an Author can do, but they can also edit and publish the posts of others - as well as their own. Editors can also invite new Contributors & Authors to the site.

Administrators
The top user level in Ghost is Administrator. Again, administrators can do everything that Authors and Editors can do, but they can also edit all site settings and data, not just content. Additionally, administrators have full access to invite, manage or remove any other user of the site.

The Owner
There is only ever one owner of a Ghost site. The owner is a special user which has all the same permissions as an Administrator, but with two exceptions: The Owner can never be deleted. You need to know what you are willing to put up with and what will cause you to immediately start looking for a new job. My mentor calls the latter "ripcord moments" because they cause you to pull the ripcord to parachute out of that situation. I recommend having these written out BEFORE shit hits the fan, although it's never too late to write these down if you're already feeling stuck.

I want to share my ripcord moments with you in the hopes that you get some use from them. They reflect what I value from my work and my team; your mileage may vary. Please also note that you don't need to have a ripcord moment to change teams - you can move due to a better opportunity, better pay, horoscope reading, whatever.

Ripcord #1: People
I want my primary projects to be with people who "fill me up" with energy when I work with them. For example, I once worked with an engineering lead who brainstormed feature ideas with me between customer meetings during an international customer tour; he and I worked together exceptionally well and it was a joy to build a service with him.

My ripcord is when I work primarily with people who drain me of energy. For example, people who go directly to my skip level manager with ideas on my space drain me of energy.

Ripcord #2: Product
I want to work on something worthwhile that also makes progress. This includes making progress on existing products as well as new endeavors.

My ripcord is seeing the same known problems repeatedly go unaddressed, and current in-flight initiatives take a longer time than expected for unknown/unspecified reasons.

Ripcord #3: Team + Leadership
This is about my directs plus my leadership chain and is really 3-in-1.

My directs: Is my team appropriately funded, do I like having them work for me (did I choose them?). I don't have a ripcord on this one because I've been blessed with directs I love.

My manager: I want to feel trusted, supported, and valued. This can come as actions taken on my behalf, sponsorship, and via project assignment. Ripcord: no actions taken to make mutually acknowledged problems go away.

My senior leadership: I want to see my leaders take feedback from the team and act on it. I want them to have a valued position within the broader organization, and I want them to be able to make hard calls and explain them. Ripcord: leaders to not take action on known issues within the team, leaders cannot explain the team direction and strategy.
