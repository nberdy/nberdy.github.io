Sparking joy

A common question I've heard from folks earlier in their careers is how you figure out what you need in a job or a manager. This often comes up in conversations around switching teams and the sorts of things one should look for when deciding to switch teams. It's a tough question to answer because the path to figuring out what to look for in a job or a manager is such an individual one. I posit that it's actually easier to give one-and-done advice on what NOT to look for in a job/career more easily than it is to coach on how to figure out what you DO want, but that tends to not be the most helpful. I have an analogy that might help.

Figuring out what you want in a job is a lot like establishing your fashion style. You have to try a whole lot of stuff before you figure out what you like, and sometimes you find it early and sometimes it feels like nothing you try really fits. Are you a person who digs the job-equivalent of a plaid flannel shirt and relaxed-fit jeans, or are you more of a fitted blazer and heels type of person? Can you mix and match components and make it work? Sometimes you just don't know until you try it on for size, and that's how you get information to make a better decision next time.

Around the time you start to really ask what you need in a job to be fulfilled, and furthermore how are you supposed to figure that out, you are going through the job equivalent of early high school fashion. It's awkward, mistakes are made, too many baggy jeans are bought, but at the end you come out the other side more confident in the choices you make.

The important thing through all of this is to be true to yourself, whoever you are at the time. If you always let someone else dress you (take the jobs other people tell you to), or always wear what looks "cool" even though you're not really into it (do what your peers do), that's how you end up unhappily in a mid-life crisis. That's when you have to do the career equivalent of Marie Kondo-ing your wardrobe, holding each aspect of your job in your hands and asking if it truly sparks joy. You might find something unexpected and switch careers to run a bed-and-breakfast in your hometown. Or you might determine that your current job only needs minor adjustments to bring you more joy.

(I am going to start using the Marie Kondo approach when having long-term career conversations and see how it goes.)
